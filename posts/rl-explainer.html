SECTION 1

The Figgie testbed

Every algorithm below is explained on one running problem: train a neural network to play Figgie well, using only games it plays against copies of itself. Before any RL, we need the game pinned down as an environment.

The game in one paragraph

Figgie is Jane Street's trading card game. A 40-card deck has four suits dealt in counts {12, 10, 10, 8} — but you don't know which suit got which count. The 12-card suit fixes the goal suit: it is the other suit of the same colour (♠–♣ are black, ♦–♥ are red). The goal suit therefore has 8 or 10 cards. Four players are dealt 10 cards each, ante \$50 into a \$200 pot, then trade single cards in an open continuous double auction for a few minutes — anyone may post a bid or an ask, or hit a standing quote. When time expires, each goal-suit card pays \$10, and whoever holds the most goal-suit cards takes the remainder of the pot. Your edge comes from inferring the goal suit faster than the table and buying it cheap.

🃏 Why Figgie is a great RL teaching domain

It hits every hard case at once: sparse reward (you only learn who won at the end), imperfect information (you never see the other hands — this is what makes CFR relevant), multi-agent non-stationarity (your opponents are also learning), and a large structured action space (post/cancel/lift quotes across four suits at sixteen prices). PPO and GRPO will tackle the first three; CFR speaks directly to the second.

Casting it as an MDP (from the learner's seat)

We train one network. From its seat, the other three players are part of the environment. Concretely, matching the figgie/ implementation:

MDP elementIn FiggieRL
State \(s_t\)The full history this seat has observed: its 10-card opening hand plus the running sequence of public market events (quotes posted, trades executed). The network is a causal Transformer over this token sequence — the tokens tensor of shape [B, T, 96]. The sequence is the state; attention is the memory.
Action \(a_t\)One of 146 discrete actions: PASS, BUY(suit), SELL(suit), QUOTE_BID(suit, price), QUOTE_ASK(suit, price) over 4 suits × 16 prices, plus cancels and MASS_CANCEL. Illegal actions are removed each step by a boolean valid_action_mask before the softmax.
Policy \(\pi_\theta(a\mid s)\)The Transformer's masked softmax head: a categorical distribution over the 146 actions, conditioned on the whole sequence so far.
Reward \(r_t\)The seat's chip delta at that step: cash paid/received on a trade, and at the final step the goal-suit payout. Everyone antes \(-\$50\) up front (chips = -50). It is sparse and end-loaded — most steps give \(0\), the score lands at the end.
Episode \(\tau\)One game: up to max_actions ≈ 100 decision steps, then score_games settles the pot.
Return \(R(\tau)\)Net chips at game end — the seat's profit. This is the thing we want to maximise in expectation.
⚠ It is really a POMDP

You never observe the opponents' hands or the true goal suit, so the "state" is a history, not a Markov snapshot of the world. We handle this the standard deep-RL way — feed the whole observed history to a sequence model and let it learn a sufficient statistic (a belief state) in its hidden activations. Keep this in your back pocket: it is precisely the gap that CFR (§8) addresses head-on with information sets instead of papering over with a recurrent/attention encoder.

What "self-play" means here

There is no fixed opponent to learn against; the environment's difficulty must rise with the learner. FiggieRL fills the other three seats from a pool: heuristic experts (TomPlayer, which prices each suit at a fair value derived from \(P(\text{goal}\mid \text{hand})\); RandomPlayer) plus frozen snapshots of the learner's own past weights, saved every snapshot_every iterations. Training against a distribution of past selves is what stops the policy from chasing one exploitable opponent and forgetting how to play in general.

✦ The one objective behind everything

Let \(\pi_\theta\) be our policy with parameters \(\theta\). Every algorithm in this document is a different way to push \(\theta\) up the same hill:

\[ J(\theta) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi_\theta}\!\big[\,R(\tau)\,\big] \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi_\theta}\!\Big[\textstyle\sum_{t} \gamma^{t} r_t\Big]. \]

We want \(\theta^\star=\arg\max_\theta J(\theta)\): the weights whose games earn the most chips on average. The trajectory distribution \(\tau\sim\pi_\theta\) depends on \(\theta\) — that dependence is the whole technical difficulty, and the next section is about getting a gradient through it.

SECTION 2

Why learn a policy, not a value

You already know the value-based recipe: learn \(Q^\star(s,a)\), then act greedily, \(\pi(s)=\arg\max_a Q(s,a)\). It is beautiful when it works. Figgie breaks three of its assumptions, and seeing exactly how motivates everything that follows.

1. The action space is large and structured.

A \(\max_a Q(s,a)\) needs a value for all 146 actions every step, and the greedy argmax gives a brittle, deterministic trader. Worse, in a betting/trading game a deterministic policy is exploitable: opponents learn you always lift the ask at \$8 and front-run you. We will need stochastic policies, which the argmax fundamentally is not.

2. Imperfect information demands randomisation.

This is the deep one. In games where you hide information, the optimal strategy is often genuinely mixed — like bluffing in poker. There is no deterministic best response to randomise against; the Nash strategy assigns positive probability to several actions in the same situation. A value function plus argmax cannot represent such a policy. A parameterised distribution \(\pi_\theta(a\mid s)\) can. (Hold this thought for §8.)

3. We want to optimise the thing we care about, directly.

Value methods optimise a surrogate (Bellman error) and hope a good policy falls out. Policy-gradient methods differentiate \(J(\theta)\) — expected chips — and step \(\theta\) straight up that gradient. The objective you train is the objective you want.

💡 The reframing

Value iteration asks "how good is each state/action?" and acts greedily. Policy gradients ask "nudge the knobs \(\theta\) so that whatever I just did, if it paid off, becomes more likely." No bootstrapped value estimate is required to define the update — though, as we'll see, a value estimate is the single best tool for making that update less noisy. That tension — pure policy gradient vs. a helper critic — is the through-line from REINFORCE to PPO.

SECTION 3

The policy gradient theorem

We want \(\nabla_\theta J(\theta)\). The obstacle is that the expectation is taken over a distribution that itself depends on \(\theta\): change the weights and you change which games get played. You cannot just push the gradient inside the expectation. The fix is a one-line identity that the entire field rests on.

The log-derivative ("score function") trick

For any distribution \(p_\theta(x)\) and function \(f\), use \(\nabla_\theta p_\theta = p_\theta \nabla_\theta \log p_\theta\):

\[ \nabla_\theta\, \mathbb{E}_{x\sim p_\theta}[f(x)] = \nabla_\theta\!\!\int p_\theta(x) f(x)\,dx = \int p_\theta(x)\,\nabla_\theta\!\log p_\theta(x)\, f(x)\,dx = \mathbb{E}_{x\sim p_\theta}\!\big[ f(x)\,\nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(x)\big]. \]

This converts "gradient of an expectation" — which we can't sample — into "expectation of a gradient", which we can: play games, evaluate the bracket on each, average. Now apply it with \(x=\tau\) and \(f=R\).

Full derivation: from \(J(\theta)\) to the score-function estimator

A trajectory's probability factorises along the chain. With environment dynamics \(p(s_{t+1}\mid s_t,a_t)\) and initial state \(\rho(s_0)\):

\[ p_\theta(\tau) = \rho(s_0)\prod_{t=0}^{T-1} \pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t)\,p(s_{t+1}\mid s_t,a_t). \]

Take logs — products become sums:

\[ \log p_\theta(\tau) = \log\rho(s_0) + \sum_{t} \log \pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t) + \sum_{t}\log p(s_{t+1}\mid s_t,a_t). \]

Differentiate w.r.t. \(\theta\). The dynamics and the initial distribution do not depend on \(\theta\), so their gradients vanish — we never need a model of the environment:

\[ \nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(\tau) = \sum_{t} \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t). \]

Substitute into the score-function identity with \(f(\tau)=R(\tau)\):

\[ \boxed{\;\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim\pi_\theta}\!\Big[\, R(\tau)\,\textstyle\sum_{t}\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t)\Big].\;} \]
✦ The policy gradient theorem
\[ \nabla_\theta J(\theta) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim\pi_\theta}\!\bigg[\, \sum_{t=0}^{T-1} \nabla_\theta \log\pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t)\;\underbrace{\Big(\sum_{t'\ge t}\gamma^{\,t'-t} r_{t'}\Big)}_{\text{return-to-go } \;\hat A_t \text{ (raw)}}\bigg]. \]

Two refinements are already baked in versus the boxed version above. (1) Causality: an action at time \(t\) can only influence future reward, so we replace the whole-episode return \(R(\tau)\) by the return-to-go \(G_t=\sum_{t'\ge t}\gamma^{t'-t}r_{t'}\). (2) That weight in front of each \(\nabla\log\pi\) is the prototype of what we'll soon call the advantage.

Reading the formula

Each term is \(\nabla_\theta \log\pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t)\) scaled by a number. The gradient \(\nabla\log\pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t)\) points in parameter space in the direction that makes action \(a_t\) more probable in state \(s_t\). Multiply by the return-to-go and sum:

  • Action led to a positive outcome (\(G_t>0\)) → step toward making it more likely.
  • Action led to a negative outcome (\(G_t<0\)) → step away, making it less likely.
  • Magnitude scales with how good/bad. That's the whole idea: reinforce what paid, suppress what cost — weighted by how much.
🃏 On the board

Mid-game your network posts QUOTE_BID(♥, 7) — a bid of \$7 for a heart. The game plays out and that seat ends +\$40. The update nudges \(\theta\) so that, in sequences that look like that one, \(\pi_\theta\) puts more mass on bidding \$7 for hearts. Notice the catch that the rest of this document exists to fix: the +\$40 is credited to every action in the game, including the irrelevant PASSes and a possibly bad heart bid that just got bailed out by good luck later. The raw signal is correct on average but drowning in noise.

SECTION 4

REINFORCE & the variance problem

Turn the theorem into an algorithm by replacing the expectation with a sample average over games you actually play. That's REINFORCE (Williams, 1992) — the Monte-Carlo policy gradient.

# REINFORCE on Figgie self-play (one iteration)
for iteration in range(num_iters):
    batch = []
    for g in range(N):                       # play N self-play games
        traj = play_game(policy, opponents=sample_pool())
        batch.append(traj)

    loss = 0
    for traj in batch:
        G = discounted_returns_to_go(traj.rewards, gamma)   # G_t for each step
        for (s, a, G_t) in zip(traj.states, traj.actions, G):
            loss = loss - log_prob(policy, s, a) * G_t   # ascent ⇒ negate
    loss = loss / N
    loss.backward(); optimizer.step()                  # θ ← θ + α·∇J

In practice: a surrogate loss, then a normal optimizer

A natural question at this point: when you implement REINFORCE, do you compute the gradient \(\nabla_\theta J\) by hand and apply it, or do you have a loss function and a normal optimizer? Almost always the latter. You build a scalar "surrogate loss" whose automatic-differentiation gradient equals the policy gradient, then call .backward() and a standard Adam/SGD step. You essentially never compute \(\nabla\log\pi\) by hand.

The mechanism: optimizer.step() wants a scalar to differentiate, not a gradient. So construct the scalar whose derivative is the policy gradient. Because the return \(G_t\) is a constant (a number from the environment), the loss

\[ L(\theta) = -\frac{1}{N}\sum_t \log\pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t)\cdot G_t \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad \nabla_\theta L = -\frac{1}{N}\sum_t \nabla_\theta\log\pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t)\cdot G_t = -\,\widehat{\nabla_\theta J} \]

has a gradient equal to the negated policy gradient. Minimising \(L\) with a normal optimizer therefore ascends \(J\) — the minus sign is only there because optimizers minimise.

# REINFORCE, the way it's actually written (PyTorch)
returns = discounted_returns_to_go(rewards, gamma)            # G_t — plain numbers
returns = (returns - returns.mean()) / (returns.std() + 1e-8)  # baseline / normalise

logits = policy(states)                                  # fresh forward pass
dist   = Categorical(logits=logits)
logp   = dist.log_prob(actions)                          # log π_θ(a_t|s_t), differentiable

loss = -(logp * returns.detach()).mean()              # the surrogate scalar

optimizer.zero_grad()
loss.backward()        # autodiff produces the policy gradient
optimizer.step()       # a completely normal Adam/SGD step
⚠ Two things that trip everyone up

1. The loss value is meaningless. This is not a supervised loss you drive toward zero. Its number, \(-\text{mean}(\log\pi\cdot G)\), drifts up, down, or sideways while training succeeds — only its gradient carries information. Don't watch loss.item() as a health signal; watch reward.

2. The weight must be detach()-ed. \(G_t\) (or, in actor–critic, the advantage) is a coefficient, not something to backprop through. Forget to detach a learned advantage and gradients leak into the critic, and the expression is no longer the policy gradient. This is exactly the new_values.detach() in your repo's ppo_loss_batched — and the same class of mistake as the entropy-detach bug in the project notes.

💡 Why not just code the gradient directly?

You could — \(\nabla_\theta\log\pi\) for a softmax has the closed form \(\text{onehot}(a)-\text{softmax}(\text{logits})\), and people did this before autodiff. But for a Transformer policy with millions of weights it is hopeless by hand. Autodiff computes it for free the instant you write log_prob(a) as a differentiable function of \(\theta\). So the universal pattern — and precisely what FiggieRL does — is build a surrogate scalar loss → .backward() → normal optimizer step. PPO and GRPO are the same pattern; they only swap \(G_t\) for a clipped, advantage-weighted term. That is why ppo_loss_batched returns a loss that flows straight into loss.backward() and the Muon/Adam step, with no manual gradients anywhere.

It is unbiased and it works — but the gradient estimate has brutally high variance, and on Figgie this is fatal without help. Two compounding reasons:

  • Sparse, noisy returns. The score only materialises at the end and swings ±\$100 on the luck of the deal. The same opening action precedes wildly different returns across games.
  • Bad credit assignment. Every action in a winning game is reinforced equally, including the mistakes. You need hundreds of games to average out which actions actually mattered.

Baselines: the single most important variance trick

Here is the lever the rest of the field pulls. Subtract a baseline \(b(s_t)\) — any function of state, not of the action — from the return:

\[ \nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}\!\Big[\textstyle\sum_t \nabla_\theta\log\pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t)\,\big(G_t - b(s_t)\big)\Big]. \]

This changes the variance but not the expectation: the estimator stays unbiased for any such \(b\). That freedom is a gift.

Why subtracting a baseline keeps the estimator unbiased

It suffices to show the extra term has zero mean. For a fixed state \(s\), summing the score over actions against a state-only baseline:

\[ \mathbb{E}_{a\sim\pi_\theta}\!\big[\nabla_\theta\log\pi_\theta(a\mid s)\,b(s)\big] = b(s)\sum_a \pi_\theta(a\mid s)\,\nabla_\theta\log\pi_\theta(a\mid s) = b(s)\sum_a \nabla_\theta \pi_\theta(a\mid s). \]

Because probabilities sum to one for every \(\theta\), \(\sum_a \pi_\theta(a\mid s)=1\Rightarrow \nabla_\theta\sum_a \pi_\theta(a\mid s)=\nabla_\theta 1 = 0\). So the whole term vanishes in expectation. We subtracted noise for free.

What is the best baseline? Intuitively, the average return you expect from state \(s\) — i.e. the value function \(V^\pi(s)\). Then the weight on each action becomes

\[ G_t - V^\pi(s_t) \;\approx\; Q^\pi(s_t,a_t) - V^\pi(s_t) \;=\; A^\pi(s_t,a_t), \]

the advantage: how much better than average was this action? A heart bid that returns +\$40 in a game where the seat expected +\$45 has negative advantage — correctly, you'd rather have done something else. Raw REINFORCE would have reinforced it.

✦ Take-away

Policy gradient with the value baseline reinforces actions by their advantage \(A^\pi(s,a)\), not their raw return. We don't know \(V^\pi\), so the obvious move is to learn it alongside the policy. That second network is the critic — and it takes us to Actor–Critic.

SECTION 5

Actor–Critic & advantage estimation

An actor–critic runs two estimators that help each other:

  • the actor \(\pi_\theta(a\mid s)\) — the policy we ultimately want;
  • the critic \(V_\phi(s)\) — a learned value function used as the baseline, so the actor can be scored by advantage instead of raw return.
🃏 In FiggieRL these share a body

The FiggieTransformer has two heads on the same trunk: a policy head emitting 146 logits and a scalar value head. The rollout records the value head's output as old_values; the loss uses it as the baseline. One network, one forward pass, two jobs — cheap and the representation is shared.

The bias–variance dial

How should the critic estimate the advantage? Two extremes:

EstimatorAdvantage of \((s_t,a_t)\)BiasVariance
Monte-Carlo (REINFORCE-style)\(G_t - V_\phi(s_t)\), full return-to-gononevery high
One-step TD (bootstrapped)\(\delta_t = r_t + \gamma V_\phi(s_{t+1}) - V_\phi(s_t)\)high (relies on \(V_\phi\))low

MC waits for the true outcome — unbiased but hostage to end-game luck. TD trusts the critic's guess of the future after one real step — low variance but biased while \(V_\phi\) is wrong. The TD error \(\delta_t\) is itself a one-sample advantage estimate.

GAE: a tunable interpolation

Generalised Advantage Estimation (Schulman et al., 2016) blends the whole spectrum with one knob \(\lambda\in[0,1]\): an exponentially-weighted sum of future TD errors.

\[ \hat A_t^{\text{GAE}(\gamma,\lambda)} \;=\; \sum_{l=0}^{\infty} (\gamma\lambda)^l\,\delta_{t+l}, \qquad \delta_t = r_t + \gamma V_\phi(s_{t+1}) - V_\phi(s_t). \]
  • \(\lambda=0\): collapses to one-step TD \(\hat A_t=\delta_t\) — low variance, biased.
  • \(\lambda=1\): telescopes to the MC advantage \(G_t - V_\phi(s_t)\) — unbiased, high variance.
  • \(0<\lambda<1\): dials in between. FiggieRL uses \(\gamma=0.99,\ \lambda=0.95\) — mostly trusting real reward, a touch of bootstrapping to tame the sparse end-loaded signal.
How that exact GAE recurrence appears in run_batched_rollouts

The sum is computed in closed form by a reverse cumulative sum over the time axis. From figgie/train.py:

next_vals = cat([old_values[:, 1:], zeros(B, 1)], dim=1)
deltas    = step_rewards + gae_gamma * next_vals - old_values   # δ_t
gl        = gae_gamma * gae_lambda                              # γλ
decay     = gl ** arange(T)                                     # (γλ)^t
gae       = (deltas * decay).flip(1).cumsum(1).flip(1) / decay   # Â_t
returns   = gae + old_values                                   # value target

The flip → cumsum → flip is the standard trick for "sum of everything from \(t\) onward". returns = Â + V recovers the bootstrapped \(\lambda\)-return used as the critic's regression target. Same formula, vectorised across a whole batch of games with no Python loop.

So far so good — but vanilla actor–critic has a dangerous habit: it takes one gradient step per batch of data, and if you make that step too big, or reuse data, the policy can collapse. That is the problem PPO was designed to solve.

SECTION 6 · KEY ALGORITHM

Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)

PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) is the workhorse of modern policy-gradient RL — and the algorithm FiggieRL actually trains with. It exists to answer one practical question: given a batch of self-play games, how do I extract as much improvement as possible without taking a step so large it destroys the policy?

The problem PPO fixes

Plain policy gradient is sample-inefficient: collect a batch, take one tiny step, throw the batch away. Rollouts are expensive (each is a full Figgie game), so we'd love to take several gradient steps on the same batch. But the moment \(\theta\) moves, the batch was generated by the old policy \(\pi_{\theta_\text{old}}\), not the current one — the data is off-policy. Push too far and the estimate becomes meaningless; the policy can lurch into garbage and never recover.

Step 1 — Importance sampling makes data reusable

The thing we actually want to push up is the advantage-weighted objective under the current policy, \(\mathbb{E}_{a_t\sim\pi_\theta}[\hat A_t]\). But during PPO's multiple update epochs every sample we hold was generated by the old policy \(\pi_{\theta_\text{old}}\) — we ran those Figgie games once and froze the batch. We have draws from \(\pi_{\theta_\text{old}}\), not from \(\pi_\theta\), so we cannot evaluate that expectation directly. Importance sampling is the standard fix: estimate an expectation under one distribution using samples from another.

The identity, for any \(p,q,f\), is to reweight each sample from \(q\) by the likelihood ratio \(p/q\):

\[ \mathbb{E}_{x\sim p}\big[f(x)\big] \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{x\sim q}\!\Big[\tfrac{p(x)}{q(x)}\,f(x)\Big]. \]

Apply it with \(p=\pi_\theta\) (what we want) and \(q=\pi_{\theta_\text{old}}\) (what we sampled from). The likelihood ratio becomes the probability ratio \(r_t(\theta)\), and the reweighted objective is the importance-sampling surrogate \(L^{\text{IS}}\):

✦ The IS surrogate objective
\[ r_t(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t)}{\pi_{\theta_\text{old}}(a_t\mid s_t)}, \qquad \underbrace{L^{\text{IS}}(\theta)}_{\text{objective to }\textbf{maximize}} = \mathbb{E}_{a_t\sim\pi_{\theta_\text{old}}}\!\big[\,r_t(\theta)\,\hat A_t\,\big] \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{a_t\sim\pi_\theta}\big[\hat A_t\big]. \]

In words: \(L^{\text{IS}}(\theta)\) is our best estimate, computed purely from the old rollout, of how good the new policy would be. Each stored sample's advantage \(\hat A_t\) is rescaled by how much more (\(r_t>1\)) or less (\(r_t<1\)) likely \(\pi_\theta\) is to have taken that action. "IS" = importance sampling; it is a quantity we climb, not a loss we minimise.

Two facts make this the right surrogate to optimise — not just a convenient guess:

  • It's exact at the start. At \(\theta=\theta_\text{old}\) every ratio \(r_t=1\), so \(L^{\text{IS}}=\mathbb{E}[\hat A_t]\) with no distortion. If the new policy makes \(a_t\) twice as likely, \(r_t=2\) and that sample counts double; half as likely, it counts half.
  • Its gradient is the policy gradient. Since \(\nabla_\theta r_t = r_t\,\nabla_\theta\log\pi_\theta\), at \(r_t=1\) we get \(\nabla_\theta L^{\text{IS}} = \mathbb{E}[\nabla_\theta\log\pi_\theta\,\hat A_t]\) — exactly the policy gradient theorem of §3. Stepping uphill on \(L^{\text{IS}}\) is, to first order, stepping uphill on real expected chips, but using reusable off-policy data.
💡 "Wait — isn't the objective \(\hat A\cdot\log\pi\)?"

A natural objection: in §4 the thing we worked with was \(\hat A\cdot\log\pi(a\mid s)\), so why is \(L^{\text{IS}}\) built from \(r_t\,\hat A\) with no \(\log\pi\) in sight? Because those are two different objects that are easy to merge:

ExpressionWhat it isIts valueIts gradient
\(\;J=\mathbb{E}[\hat A]\;\)the objective (expected chips)meaningful\(\mathbb{E}[\nabla\log\pi\,\hat A]\)
\(\;\mathbb{E}[\log\pi\cdot\hat A]\;\) (\(\hat A\) detached)the REINFORCE surrogate lossmeaningless\(\mathbb{E}[\nabla\log\pi\,\hat A]\) — identical

The \(\hat A\cdot\log\pi\) you remember was never the objective; it was the surrogate loss — an expression engineered so autodiff spits out \(\nabla J\) (exactly the "its value is meaningless, only its gradient matters" box back in §4). The real objective has always been the plain expected advantage \(\mathbb{E}[\hat A]\), and \(L^{\text{IS}}=\mathbb{E}[r_t\,\hat A]\) is just that same objective rewritten with importance sampling — not a new surrogate loss.

So why \(r_t\,\hat A\) and not \(\log\pi\cdot\hat A\) as the thing to climb over several steps?

  • \(\log\pi\cdot\hat A\) only has the correct gradient at \(\theta_\text{old}\). It's one-shot: its value doesn't track the updated policy at all, so after a single step it's stale and there's nothing meaningful left to maximise.
  • \(r_t\,\hat A\) stays an honest, evaluable estimate of the new policy's performance for any \(\theta\), because \(r_t=\pi_\theta/\pi_{\theta_\text{old}}\) keeps tracking how far \(\pi_\theta\) has drifted. That is precisely what lets PPO take several gradient steps on one frozen batch — the whole reason the algorithm exists.

And they're consistent: from \(\nabla r_t = r_t\,\nabla\log\pi\) and \(r_t=1\) at \(\theta_\text{old}\), both forms share the same gradient at the current policy. They differ only away from \(\theta_\text{old}\) — where \(r_t\,\hat A\) stays meaningful and \(\log\pi\cdot\hat A\) does not.

The catch — and the entire reason PPO exists. Importance sampling is only trustworthy while \(\pi_\theta\) and \(\pi_{\theta_\text{old}}\) stay close. As \(r_t\) wanders far from 1, the estimator's variance explodes, and \(L^{\text{IS}}\) starts handing out big rewards for dumping probability mass onto whichever action has the noisiest advantage estimate rather than the genuinely best one — so maximising it blindly can lurch the policy into garbage. We need a leash on \(r_t\).

Step 2 — The clipped surrogate objective

TRPO leashed it with a hard KL-divergence constraint and second-order optimisation. PPO's insight is that you can get the same "don't move too far" effect with a brutally simple first-order trick: clip the ratio so the objective stops rewarding you once you've moved far enough.

✦ PPO clipped objective
\[ L^{\text{CLIP}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_t\Big[\,\min\big(\,r_t(\theta)\,\hat A_t,\;\; \operatorname{clip}(r_t(\theta),\,1-\epsilon,\,1+\epsilon)\,\hat A_t\,\big)\Big]. \]

FiggieRL uses \(\epsilon=0.2\) (ppo_clip_eps). The min of the unclipped and clipped terms makes the bound pessimistic — it always takes the more conservative of the two.

Reading the clip, case by case

This is the heart of PPO; it's worth getting it in your bones. Consider one sample.

  • Advantage positive (\(\hat A_t>0\)) — a good action. We want to raise its probability, so \(r_t\) climbs above 1. The objective increases until \(r_t=1+\epsilon\); past that the clipped term flattens it. No reward for pushing a good action's probability up more than ~20%. The gradient there is zero — the incentive to keep moving dies.
  • Advantage negative (\(\hat A_t<0\)) — a bad action. We want to lower its probability, so \(r_t\) drops below 1. The objective improves until \(r_t=1-\epsilon\), then flattens. No reward for crushing a bad action below ~−20% in one update.
  • The asymmetric min has a subtle, important role: if a step overshoots (a positive-advantage action's ratio somehow lands below 1, or vice-versa), the unclipped term is selected and the full gradient pulls it back. The clip caps gains but never caps corrections.

Step 3 — The full PPO loss

The actual training loss adds the critic's regression and an entropy bonus:

\[ L(\theta,\phi) \;=\; \underbrace{-\,L^{\text{CLIP}}(\theta)}_{\text{policy (actor)}} \;+\; \underbrace{c_v\, \mathbb{E}_t\big[(V_\phi(s_t)-\hat R_t)^2\big]}_{\text{value (critic)}} \;-\; \underbrace{c_e\,\mathbb{E}_t\big[\mathcal H[\pi_\theta(\cdot\mid s_t)]\big]}_{\text{entropy bonus}}. \]
  • Value loss regresses the critic onto the GAE \(\lambda\)-returns \(\hat R_t\). FiggieRL: vf_coef = 0.5.
  • Entropy bonus rewards a less peaked policy, sustaining exploration and — crucially in an adversarial game — keeping the policy mixed and hard to exploit. FiggieRL: entropy_coef = 0.03. project note An earlier bug detached entropy from the graph and the policy collapsed to near-deterministic overconfidence; the coefficient and a correct gradient path are what hold it open.

The PPO training loop, FiggieRL-style

for iteration in range(num_iters):
    # ── 1. COLLECT: run a big batch of self-play games on GPU ──
    opps = sample_pool(experts + snapshots)
    exp  = run_batched_rollouts(model, opps, num_games=1024, G=8,
                                gae_gamma=0.99, gae_lambda=0.95)
    # records: old_log_probs, old_values, returns (GAE), valid_masks …

    # ── 2. OPTIMISE: several epochs of minibatch updates on the SAME batch ──
    for step in range(train_steps):          # reuse the rollout ~epochs times
        mb = sample_minibatch(exp, minibatch_size=256)
        logits, new_values = model(mb.tokens)        # fresh forward pass
        ratio = exp((new_lp - mb.old_log_probs))     # r_t(θ)
        adv   = normalize(mb.returns - new_values.detach())
        surr1 = ratio * adv
        surr2 = clamp(ratio, 1-0.2, 1+0.2) * adv
        pg    = -min(surr1, surr2).mean()            # clipped policy loss
        vf    = (new_values - mb.returns).pow(2).mean()
        ent   = entropy(logits).mean()
        loss  = pg + 0.5*vf - 0.03*ent
        loss.backward(); clip_grad(1.0); muon_adam_step()

    if iteration % snapshot_every == 0:
        pool.add(freeze(model))                       # self-play opponent
How this maps to ppo_loss_batched in the repo (the real details)

A few choices in figgie/train.py worth flagging, because they differ from the textbook sketch:

  • Action masking everywhere. Both at rollout and in the loss, illegal-action logits are set to \(-\infty\) before log_softmax, so probability mass and entropy are computed over legal actions only.
  • Only the model's own steps count. A game's token stream interleaves all four seats; model_acts masks the loss/entropy/advantage to the steps the learner actually chose. Opponent moves are context, not training signal.
  • Returns are normalised across the minibatch (R_norm = (returns − mean)/std) before forming the advantage, and the advantage is normalised again. Whitening keeps the gradient scale stable across iterations regardless of the pot's raw chip magnitude.
  • Advantage uses the live critic: adv = R_norm − new_values.detach(). The detach stops advantage gradients from leaking into the value head — the critic is trained only by the value loss, the actor only by the policy loss.
  • Optimiser: Muon (Newton–Schulz-orthogonalised momentum) on 2-D weight matrices, AdamW on the rest, gradient-clipped at 1.0.
⚠ Diagnostics that tell you PPO is healthy

The loss records clip_frac (fraction of samples hitting the clip — want a modest, non-zero value; near-1 means the step or learning rate is too hot), explained_var (is the critic actually predicting returns? near 0 or negative means a useless baseline → variance back to REINFORCE levels), ratio_mean (should hover near 1), and avg_max_prob (creeping toward 1.0 = the overconfidence collapse the entropy bonus exists to prevent). Watch these, not just reward.

💡 PPO in one sentence

Collect self-play games once, then squeeze several safe gradient steps out of them by reweighting with the probability ratio and clipping that ratio so no single update can move the policy more than ~20% — turning the expensive, fragile policy gradient into a cheap, stable, reusable one.

SECTION 7 · KEY ALGORITHM

Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO)

GRPO (DeepSeek, 2024) keeps PPO's clipped surrogate but throws the critic away. It was designed for RLHF on language models, where training a good value head is hard — but its core idea is a perfect fit for a competitive, relative-reward game like Figgie, and the figgie/ config already carries its central knob: group_size G = 8.

The idea: let a group be its own baseline

Recall the baseline's only job (§4) is to answer "compared to what?" — to convert a raw return into "better or worse than expected." PPO learns a critic \(V_\phi(s)\) to supply that "expected." GRPO's move is almost cheeky: instead of learning the expected value of a situation, sample the situation many times and use the group's own average as the baseline.

For a given starting situation, draw a group of \(G\) trajectories \(\{o_1,\dots,o_G\}\) from the current policy, score each by its return \(R_i\), and define a group-relative advantage by standardising within the group:

\[ \hat A_i \;=\; \frac{R_i - \operatorname{mean}(R_1,\dots,R_G)}{\operatorname{std}(R_1,\dots,R_G)}. \]

That's the whole baseline. No value network, no GAE, no bootstrapping. An action sequence that beat its group's average gets positive advantage; one that lagged gets negative. Then plug \(\hat A_i\) into the exact same clipped surrogate as PPO.

✦ GRPO objective
\[ L^{\text{GRPO}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}\Big[\, \min\big(r_i(\theta)\hat A_i,\ \operatorname{clip}(r_i(\theta),1-\epsilon,1+\epsilon)\hat A_i\big) \;-\; \beta\, D_{\text{KL}}\!\big(\pi_\theta \,\|\, \pi_{\text{ref}}\big)\Big] \]

with the group-standardised \(\hat A_i\) above. Two structural differences from PPO: (1) no \(c_v\) value-loss term — there is no critic to train; (2) the KL to a fixed reference policy is often added directly to the loss as a penalty (rather than only implied by clipping), estimated with the unbiased low-variance estimator \(D_{\text{KL}}\approx \tfrac{\pi_{\text{ref}}}{\pi_\theta}-\log\tfrac{\pi_{\text{ref}}}{\pi_\theta}-1\).

Why this is uncannily natural for Figgie

In language-model RLHF, the "group" is \(G\) different answers sampled for the same prompt, and the relative advantage says "which answers were better than the others for this prompt." Translate to Figgie:

🃏 A group = the same deal, played out G times

Deal one Figgie hand, then have the policy play that situation forward \(G=8\) times (different sampled actions, and — in FiggieRL's batched rollout — rotated through the four seats so each group covers the table symmetrically). You get eight final chip counts. Subtract the group mean: now "+\$30 in a deal where the group averaged +\$50" is correctly a below-average outcome, even though it's a profit. The luck of which goal suit was dealt is largely shared across the group, so subtracting the group mean cancels the deal's variance and isolates the part of the outcome due to skill of play. That is exactly the signal you want — and Figgie's reward is already a relative, zero-sum-ish quantity, so a relative baseline is arguably more honest than an absolute learned \(V_\phi\).

💡 The unifying picture

PPO, A2C and GRPO are the same clipped policy-gradient skeleton with different advantage estimators bolted on. A2C: one-step TD. PPO: GAE off a learned critic. GRPO: standardise within a group of siblings. Once you see "it's all about how you compute \(\hat A_t\) and how hard you clip \(r_t\)," every one of these algorithms becomes a two-line variation on the policy gradient theorem from §3.

FiggieRL is a hybrid — and seeing why is instructive

The repo isn't dogmatically one or the other. It keeps a value head and GAE (PPO-style fine-grained credit assignment across the ~100 trading decisions in a game) and carries group_size = 8 with seat-rotated groups (GRPO-style structure that cancels deal luck), normalising returns across the batch. That's a deliberate engineering choice: per-step credit matters in Figgie because a single mispriced quote 80 steps before the end genuinely is a localised mistake — pure outcome-level GRPO would smear blame across the whole game. The group structure is layered on top to kill the dominant variance source (the deal). Understanding both algorithms is what lets you read that design as the considered compromise it is, rather than a muddle.

SECTION 8 · KEY ALGORITHM

Counterfactual Regret Minimization (CFR)

PPO and GRPO are gradient methods: they nudge a policy uphill and hope. They have no built-in notion that Figgie is a game of hidden information against adversaries, and nothing guarantees they converge to an unexploitable strategy — left alone, two self-playing PPO agents can chase each other in circles. CFR comes from a different lineage — game theory — and was the engine behind superhuman poker (Libratus, Pluribus). It is built from the ground up for exactly Figgie's defining feature: imperfect information. This section is the conceptual payoff; it requires a small shift in formalism.

New formalism: extensive-form games and information sets

Drop the MDP picture; adopt the extensive-form game (a game tree). The key new object is the information set \(I\): the set of game states (tree nodes) that a player cannot tell apart from what they've observed.

🃏 An information set in Figgie

You hold {3♥, 2♦, …} and have watched a certain sequence of quotes and trades. Many true world-states are consistent with that — every possible arrangement of the 30 cards you can't see, every possible goal suit not yet ruled out. All those distinct tree nodes form one information set \(I\): the situations you experience as identical. A strategy can only condition on \(I\), never on the hidden truth — so it must assign one distribution over actions per information set. (This is the honest version of the POMDP hand-wave from §1: where PPO encodes the history into a vector and hopes, CFR reasons over the information set explicitly.)

A strategy \(\sigma_i\) maps each info set \(I\) to a distribution \(\sigma_i(I)\) over its legal actions. The solution concept is a Nash equilibrium: a strategy profile where no player can gain by unilaterally deviating — i.e. an unexploitable way to play. CFR is an algorithm that provably converges to one in two-player zero-sum games.

The engine: regret matching

Forget gradients. CFR is built on regret: "how much better would I have done, in hindsight, had I always played action \(a\) here instead of my actual mixed strategy?" The algorithm is a loop:

  1. Play the current strategy through the tree.
  2. At every information set, compute the regret of each action — counterfactually.
  3. Accumulate those regrets over all iterations.
  4. Set the next strategy proportional to positive accumulated regret. Actions you wish you'd played more get more probability.

Counterfactual value

"Counterfactual" is the load-bearing word. The counterfactual value of info set \(I\) for player \(i\) weights each reachable terminal \(z\) by the probability that everyone else (and chance) plays to reach it — but treats player \(i\) as if they deliberately aimed to reach \(I\) (its own reach probability is factored out). This isolates "how good is being here, and what can I do about it," independent of how likely \(i\)'s own past play made reaching \(I\):

\[ v_i(\sigma, I) \;=\; \sum_{z\in Z_I} \pi^\sigma_{-i}(z)\,\pi^\sigma(z[I]\!\to\! z)\,u_i(z), \]

where \(\pi^\sigma_{-i}\) is the reach probability from everyone but \(i\), \(u_i(z)\) the payoff (chips), and \(Z_I\) the terminals through \(I\).

Counterfactual regret & the update

The counterfactual regret of action \(a\) at \(I\) on iteration \(t\) is how much more counterfactual value you'd have gotten by committing to \(a\):

\[ r^t(I,a) \;=\; v_i\big(\sigma^t_{I\to a},\,I\big) - v_i\big(\sigma^t, I\big), \qquad R^T(I,a) = \sum_{t=1}^{T} r^t(I,a). \]

Then regret matching sets the next strategy proportional to positive cumulative regret (uniform if nothing is positive):

\[ \sigma^{T+1}(I,a) = \frac{R^{T,+}(I,a)}{\sum_b R^{T,+}(I,b)}, \qquad R^{+}=\max(R,0). \]
⚠ The strategy that converges is the AVERAGE, not the last one

This trips everyone up. The per-iteration strategies \(\sigma^t\) oscillate forever and do not individually approach equilibrium. The object that converges to Nash is the average strategy \(\bar\sigma^T(I,a)\propto\sum_t \pi_i^{\sigma^t}(I)\,\sigma^t(I,a)\) — the running, reach-weighted average of all strategies played. You must accumulate two tables per info set: cumulative regret (drives the next strategy) and the cumulative strategy (averaged at the end to read off the answer).

Why minimising regret yields Nash (the one theorem to remember)

Regret matching is a no-regret online-learning rule: at each info set, average regret \(\frac1T\sum_t r^t(I,a)\to 0\) as \(T\to\infty\) (it's bounded by \(O(1/\sqrt T)\)). The counterfactual construction's key lemma is that a player's total regret is bounded by the sum of their per-information-set counterfactual regrets — so minimising regret locally minimises it globally. Finally, a folk theorem of online learning: in a two-player zero-sum game, if both players have average regret \(\to 0\), their average strategies form a Nash equilibrium (the average exploitability is bounded by the sum of the two average regrets). Chain those three facts and you have CFR's guarantee.

A concrete pass on micro-Figgie

🃏 One regret-matching update, by hand

Strip Figgie to a toy: at some information set \(I\) you may BUY ♥, SELL ♥, or PASS. Start uniform, \(\sigma^1=(\tfrac13,\tfrac13,\tfrac13)\). You traverse the tree and find the counterfactual values \(v(\text{buy})=+6\), \(v(\text{sell})=-2\), \(v(\text{pass})=0\), so the strategy's value is \(\tfrac13(6-2+0)=+1.33\). The instantaneous regrets are \(r(\text{buy})=6-1.33=+4.67\), \(r(\text{sell})=-2-1.33=-3.33\), \(r(\text{pass})=0-1.33=-1.33\). Only buy has positive cumulative regret, so

σ² = (BUY 1.0, SELL 0.0, PASS 0.0) — pure buy next iteration.

Next iteration the world (and opponents) shift, buying ♥ may now be regretted, and the mix swings back. Over thousands of iterations the regrets balance and the average strategy settles into a stable, mixed, unexploitable way to trade hearts in that situation — perhaps (BUY 0.55, PASS 0.35, SELL 0.10). That mixing is the bluff-like randomisation §2 promised you couldn't get from an argmax.

Making CFR practical: sampling & variants

Vanilla CFR traverses the entire game tree every iteration — impossible for full Figgie (a continuous double auction has an astronomically large tree). The fixes:

  • Monte-Carlo CFR (MCCFR). Sample trajectories instead of full traversal. External sampling samples chance and opponents' actions, enumerating only the current player's; outcome sampling samples a single trajectory. Unbiased estimates of counterfactual regret, dramatically cheaper.
  • CFR+. Floor cumulative regrets at zero (regret-matching+) and weight the strategy average linearly toward later iterations. Converges much faster in practice; powered the poker breakthroughs.
  • Abstraction. Bucket similar information sets (similar hands/market states) into one, solve the smaller game, map back. Necessary to fit a game like Figgie into memory at all.
  • Deep CFR. Replace the giant regret/strategy tables with neural networks that generalise across information sets — closing the loop back toward the function approximators PPO uses, but trained on the regret objective.

✦ CFR in one breath

Track, at every information set, how much you regret not having favoured each action; play next in proportion to positive cumulative regret; and report the time-average strategy. In two-player zero-sum games that average provably converges to an unexploitable Nash equilibrium — giving you, unlike a raw policy gradient, a guarantee that no opponent can systematically beat your Figgie play.

SECTION 9 · KEY ALGORITHM

Deep CFR: CFR meets function approximation

Tabular CFR has a fatal scaling problem on a game like Figgie, and Deep CFR (Brown, Lerer, Gross & Sandholm, 2019) is the fix that finally lets CFR's equilibrium guarantee ride on the same neural machinery that PPO uses. This chapter is where the two halves of the document — game-theoretic regret minimisation and deep function approximation — finally fuse.

The wall tabular CFR hits

Recall §8: CFR keeps, for every information set \(I\), two tables — cumulative regret \(R(I,a)\) and the cumulative strategy. That is fine for toy games. Full Figgie is hopeless:

  • An information set is "your hand + the entire observed market history." The number of distinct hands alone is enormous, and each is multiplied by every possible sequence of quotes and trades you could have watched.
  • You would need to visit each information set many times to estimate its regret — but the vast majority are never seen twice, so the tables never fill in.

The classical workaround is abstraction: hand-design buckets that lump "similar" hands and market states into one information set, solve the shrunken game, map back. It works (it powered early poker bots) but it is lossy, brittle, and demands deep domain expertise to design the buckets. Deep CFR's thesis: don't hand-design buckets — let a neural network learn to generalise across information sets automatically.

💡 The one-line idea

Deep CFR is to CFR what deep \(Q\)-learning is to tabular \(Q\)-learning, and what PPO's policy net is to a tabular policy: replace the giant lookup tables with function approximators. Instead of storing a regret number per \((I,a)\) cell, train a network \(R_\psi(I)\) that predicts the regret vector from features of \(I\). Two hands that play similarly get similar predictions without either being visited — generalisation does the work abstraction used to.

The two networks

Deep CFR trains (per player \(p\)) two kinds of network, replacing the two CFR tables:

  • Advantage / regret network \(R_\psi^p(I,a)\). Takes the features of an information set, outputs a value per legal action that approximates the cumulative counterfactual regret (more precisely its iteration-average — regret matching is scale-invariant, so predicting the average is equivalent and numerically nicer). The current strategy at any \(I\) is just regret matching on this network's output: \(\sigma(I,a)\propto R_\psi^p(I,a)^{+}\).
  • Average-strategy network \(\Pi_\phi(I,a)\). Approximates the running average strategy \(\bar\sigma\) — the object that actually converges to Nash (the §8 pitfall: it's the average, never the last iterate). This network is the final deliverable you deploy to play Figgie.

The training loop

Because traversing the whole tree is impossible, Deep CFR rides on external-sampling Monte-Carlo CFR (§8): sample chance and the opponents' moves, enumerate only the traverser's actions. Each CFR iteration \(t\):

  1. Traverse. For the traverser \(p\), run \(K\) external-sampling traversals. The strategy used at each visited \(I\) is regret matching on the current advantage network \(R_\psi^p\).
  2. Collect regret targets. At every information set visited, compute the sampled instantaneous counterfactual regret vector \(\tilde r^t(I,\cdot)\), and push the sample \(\big(I,\ t,\ \tilde r^t(I,\cdot)\big)\) into a fixed-size advantage memory \(\mathcal M_v^p\) using reservoir sampling (so the buffer always holds a uniform sample of everything seen so far, despite finite size).
  3. Collect strategy targets. Push \(\big(I,\ t,\ \sigma^t(I,\cdot)\big)\) into a separate strategy memory \(\mathcal M_\Pi\) — the raw material for the average-strategy network.
  4. Retrain from scratch. Re-initialise \(R_\psi^p\) and fit it (regression) to the regrets in \(\mathcal M_v^p\), weighting each sample by its iteration \(t\) (this is the Linear CFR weighting — later iterations matter more). Training from scratch each time is deliberate: the target is a cumulative quantity that shifts, and warm-starting biases the fit.

After \(T\) iterations, train \(\Pi_\phi\) once on \(\mathcal M_\Pi\) (again \(t\)-weighted) to recover \(\bar\sigma\). That network is your approximate Nash strategy.

# Deep CFR, one player's iteration (external sampling)
for t in range(T):
    for p in players:
        for k in range(K):                     # K sampled traversals
            traverse(root, traverser=p,
                     strategy=lambda I: regret_match(R_psi[p](I)),  # σ from net
                     on_visit=lambda I, r_hat: M_v[p].add((I, t, r_hat)),   # reservoir
                     on_strat=lambda I, sigma: M_pi.add((I, t, sigma)))
        R_psi[p] = train_from_scratch(M_v[p], weight_by=t)      # Linear CFR

avg_strategy_net = train_from_scratch(M_pi, weight_by=t)        # ← deploy this
🃏 What this buys you on Figgie

You never enumerate Figgie's information sets. You play sampled games; at each decision the advantage network reads your hand + market history and outputs a regret estimate per action (BUY ♥, QUOTE_ASK(♦,8), …); regret matching turns that into the mixed strategy you actually play; and the visited situations become regression targets. A hand you have never held gets a sensible regret prediction because it looks like hands the net has seen — the generalisation that hand-built abstraction could only approximate. Crucially, you can reuse the very same FiggieTransformer trunk from the PPO setup: same token-sequence encoder of the game history, but with a regret-regression head and a CFR target instead of a clipped policy-gradient loss.

⚠ The catches

Deep CFR's convergence guarantee is now approximate — it degrades with the networks' regression error, so a badly-fit advantage net no longer provably approaches Nash. It needs large reservoir memories and a full network re-fit every iteration (expensive). And the two-network bookkeeping (advantage + average-strategy, per player) is heavier than a single PPO policy. These costs are why later work compressed it: Single Deep CFR drops the separate average-strategy net; DREAM and ARMAC fold the regret estimate into an actor–critic-style single-trajectory method, pulling Deep CFR much closer to the policy-gradient world you already know.

✦ Deep CFR in one breath

Run Monte-Carlo CFR, but store the regrets it produces as training data and learn a network to predict regret from any information set — so generalisation replaces hand-built abstraction, and a game far too large to tabulate (like real Figgie) becomes tractable. You keep CFR's prize — convergence toward an unexploitable strategy — while gaining the scalability of the deep nets that power PPO. It is, quite literally, the bridge between the two families this document has been building toward.

SECTION 10

So which do you use for Figgie?

You now have three tools and a feel for their shapes. The honest answer is that they occupy different niches, and a strong Figgie agent might borrow from all of them.

The case for PPO / GRPO (what FiggieRL does)

The action space is large and structured, the state is a long history best handled by a Transformer, and you want to scale with compute. Policy gradients eat all of this natively. PPO gives you stable, sample-reusable updates; the group structure of GRPO cancels the dominant variance source (the deal). With a self-play snapshot pool to keep the opponent distribution rich, this is the pragmatic, scalable path — and it's exactly the design in figgie/. Its weakness is principled: it chases a moving target and carries no guarantee of unexploitability.

The case for CFR

If your goal is a provably hard-to-exploit strategy and you're willing to abstract Figgie down to a tractable game, CFR (CFR+/MCCFR, or Deep CFR to keep the neural generalisation) targets the right object — Nash — and treats hidden information as a first-class citizen rather than something to encode and hope about. Its weakness is equally principled: full Figgie's game tree forces heavy abstraction, and "unexploitable" is not the same as "maximally exploits weak opponents."

💡 The honest synthesis

They optimise different things. PPO/GRPO maximise expected profit against the opponents you actually train on — superb for exploiting a known field, at the risk of being exploited back. CFR minimises worst-case exploitability — a safety floor, sometimes leaving money on the table against bad players. The frontier of game AI (e.g. ReBeL, player-of-games) combines them: CFR-style equilibrium reasoning for soundness, deep nets and gradient learning for scale. For FiggieRL today, PPO+GAE with GRPO-style grouped self-play is the right engine; understanding CFR tells you precisely what guarantee that engine is and isn't giving you.
